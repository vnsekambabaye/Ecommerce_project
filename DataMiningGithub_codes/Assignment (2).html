Question 2

CarefullyexplainthedifferencesbetweentheKNNclassifierandKNN regressionmethods.

Answer:

  • What is KNN?: It predicts an outcome for a new data point by looking at the K closest data points (neighbors) in the training data, based on some distance measure (e.g., Euclidean distance). The difference lies in how it handles the outcome variable, depending on whether its a classification or regression problem.
  • Key Differences:

1. Type of Problem:

  • KNN Classifier: Used for classification problems, where the outcome is categorical (e.g., success or failure). It predicts the class of a new data point by finding the K nearest neighbors and choosing the most common class among them (majority vote). For example, if K=3 and two neighbors are success and one is failure, it predicts success.
  • KNN Regression: Used for regression problems, where the outcome is numerical (e.g., a cars mpg). It predicts the value by averaging the numerical outcomes of the K nearest neighbors. For example, if K=3 and the neighbors have mpg values of 20, 22, and 24, the prediction is (20 + 22 + 24) / 3 = 22.


2. Output:

  • KNN Classifier: Outputs a category or class label (e.g., yes or no). If theres a tie in the majority vote, it may use rules like picking the class with the closest neighbor.
  • KNN Regression: Outputs a continuous number, calculated as the average (or sometimes weighted average) of the neighbors values.


3. How Neighbors Contribute:

  • KNN Classifier: Each neighbor votes for its class, and the class with the most votes wins. The prediction is discrete.
  • KNN Regression: Each neighbor contributes its numerical value to an average, smoothing the prediction across neighbors.


4. Performance Metrics :

  • KNN Classifier: Accuracy is measured by error rate (e.g., percentage of incorrect classifications, section 2.2.3).
  • KNN Regression: Accuracy is measured by metrics like mean squared error (MSE), which calculates the average squared difference between predicted and actual values .

5. Example from Auto Dataset:

  • Classifier: If we predict whether a cars origin is US (1) or non-US (2, 3) based on weight and horsepower, KNN classifier finds the K nearest cars and picks the most common origin.
  • Regression: If we predict mpg based on weight and horsepower, KNN regression averages the mpg of the K nearest cars.
  • Intuitive Analogy: Imagine youre guessing someones house price (regression) or whether they own a cat or dog (classification). For regression, you look at the K nearest houses and average their prices. For classification, you check the K nearest neighbors and see if most have cats or dogs, then pick the majority.


Answer:
The KNN classifier is used for categorical outcomes, predicting the most common class among the K nearest neighbors via majority vote. KNN regression is for numerical outcomes, predicting the average value of the K nearest neighbors. The classifier outputs a discrete category, while regression outputs a continuous number. Their performance is evaluated differently (error rate for classification, MSE for regression).

Question 7: Prove $R^2$ Equals the Square of the Correlation in Simple Linear Regression

Task:
Prove that in simple linear regression of $Y$ onto $X$, with the assumption that the mean of $X$ and $Y$ is zero ($\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$), the $R^2$ statistic (equation 3.17) equals the square of the correlation between $X$ and $Y$ (equation 3.18).

Explanation:

Simple Linear Regression:
This is when we predict $Y$ (e.g., mpg) using one predictor $X$ (e.g., weight), with a model like:
$Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \epsilon$ (section 3.1).
Here, we assume no intercept ($\beta_0 = 0$) since $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$, simplifying the model to:
$Y = \beta_1 X + \epsilon$



$R^2$:
The $R^2$ statistic (equation 3.17) measures how well the regression line explains the variation in $Y$. It's defined as:
$$ R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SSR}}{\text{SST}} $$
where:


  • SSR (Sum of Squared Residuals):
    $\sum (Y_i - \hat{Y}_i)^2$

  • SST (Total Sum of Squares):
    $\sum (Y_i - \bar{y})^2$
    Since $\bar{y} = 0$, then SST = $\sum Y_i^2$

Correlation:
The correlation coefficient (equation 3.18) measures the linear relationship between $X$ and $Y$:
$$ r = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sqrt{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2}} $$
Since $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$, the formula simplifies (no need to subtract means).

Goal: Show that $R^2 = r^2$

Proof (Simplified for Non-Math Background):

Model Setup:
In simple linear regression without an intercept ($\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$), the slope $\hat{\beta}_1$ is:
$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum X_i^2} $$
Predicted values:
$\hat{Y}_i = \hat{\beta}_1 X_i$

SSR Calculation:
Residuals: $Y_i - \hat{Y}_i = Y_i - \hat{\beta}_1 X_i$
So:
$$ \text{SSR} = \sum (Y_i - \hat{\beta}_1 X_i)^2 $$

SST Calculation:
Since $\bar{y} = 0$, then:
$$ \text{SST} = \sum Y_i^2 $$

Express $R^2$: $$ R^2 = 1 - \frac{\text{SSR}}{\text{SST}} = 1 - \frac{\sum (Y_i - \hat{\beta}_1 X_i)^2}{\sum Y_i^2} $$

Substitute $\hat{\beta}_1$:
$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum X_i^2} $$
So:
$$ \hat{Y}_i = \left( \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum X_i^2} \right) X_i $$

Compute ESS instead (SST - SSR): $$ \text{ESS} = \sum (\hat{Y}_i)^2 = \sum (\hat{\beta}_1 X_i)^2 = \hat{\beta}_1^2 \sum X_i^2 $$ Substitute $\hat{\beta}_1$:
$$ \text{ESS} = \left( \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum X_i^2} \right)^2 \sum X_i^2 = \frac{(\sum X_i Y_i)^2}{\sum X_i^2} $$

So: $$ R^2 = \frac{\text{ESS}}{\text{SST}} = \frac{\frac{(\sum X_i Y_i)^2}{\sum X_i^2}}{\sum Y_i^2} = \frac{(\sum X_i Y_i)^2}{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2} $$

Correlation Squared: $$ r = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sqrt{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2}}, \quad r^2 = \left( \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sqrt{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2}} \right)^2 = \frac{(\sum X_i Y_i)^2}{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2} $$

Conclusion:
Comparing the two: $$ R^2 = \frac{(\sum X_i Y_i)^2}{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2} = r^2 $$
Thus, $R^2 = r^2$

Intuitive Explanation:
$R^2$ tells you how much of $Y$s variation is explained by $X$.
The correlation $r$ measures how closely $X$ and $Y$ move together linearly.
When $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$, the regression lines fit (how well $X$ predicts $Y$) matches the strength of their linear relationship, so squaring the correlation gives $R^2$.

Answer:
In simple linear regression with $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$, $R^2$ equals the square of the correlation between $X$ and $Y$, as shown by the equality of their formulas:
$$ R^2 = \frac{(\sum X_i Y_i)^2}{\sum X_i^2 \sum Y_i^2} = r^2 $$

Question 12: Simple Linear Regression Without an Intercept

Task:
This problem involves simple linear regression without an intercept.

(a) Under What Circumstance is the Coefficient Estimate for $X$ onto $Y$ the Same as $Y$ onto $X$?

Explanation:
In simple linear regression without an intercept, the model is:
$$ Y = \beta X + \epsilon $$
(from section 3.1).
The coefficient $\hat{\beta}$ for regressing $Y$ onto $X$ is (equation 3.38):
$$ \hat{\beta}_{Y|X} = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum X_i^2} $$

For regressing $X$ onto $Y$, the model is:
$$ X = \beta Y + \epsilon $$
and the coefficient is:
$$ \hat{\beta}_{X|Y} = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum Y_i^2} $$

We need to find when:
$$ \hat{\beta}_{Y|X} = \hat{\beta}_{X|Y} $$

Derivation:
Set the two expressions equal: $$ \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum X_i^2} = \frac{\sum X_i Y_i}{\sum Y_i^2} $$
Assuming $\sum X_i Y_i \neq 0$ (otherwise, no relationship exists), divide both sides by $\sum X_i Y_i$: $$ \frac{1}{\sum X_i^2} = \frac{1}{\sum Y_i^2} $$
This implies: $$ \sum X_i^2 = \sum Y_i^2 $$

Interpretation:
This condition means the sum of squared values of $X$ equals the sum of squared values of $Y$.
In statistical terms, this occurs when the variances of $X$ and $Y$ are equal assuming centered data (i.e., $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$), as in Question 7.

Answer:
The coefficient estimates for $X$ onto $Y$ and $Y$ onto $X$ are the same when:
$$ \sum X_i^2 = \sum Y_i^2 $$
i.e., when the sum of squared $X$ values equals the sum of squared $Y$ values.
